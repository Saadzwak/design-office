# Neuroarchitecture in the office

This document distils the design-relevant evidence from environmental
psychology, restorative design, and neuroarchitecture into actionable cues for
a 21st-century office fit-out.

## 1. Biophilic design — foundational framework

### 1.1 Browning et al. 2014 — 14 Patterns of Biophilic Design

Browning, W. D., Ryan, C. O., & Clancy, J. O. (2014). **14 Patterns of Biophilic
Design**. Terrapin Bright Green LLC, New York. A 10th-anniversary edition was
published in 2024.

The 14 patterns group into **three experiential categories** :

| Category                     | Patterns                                                                                     |
|------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Nature in the space**      | 1 Visual connection with nature · 2 Non-visual connection · 3 Non-rhythmic sensory stimuli · 4 Thermal & airflow variability · 5 Presence of water · 6 Dynamic & diffuse light · 7 Connection with natural systems |
| **Natural analogues**        | 8 Biomorphic forms & patterns · 9 Material connection with nature · 10 Complexity & order                                          |
| **Nature of the space**      | 11 Prospect · 12 Refuge · 13 Mystery · 14 Risk / peril                                                                              |

Each pattern has documented physiological, cognitive and psychological
benefits. Key planning implications :

- **Visual connection with nature (P1)** — a sight-line to greenery or
  landscape from every regularly occupied seat. Typical target : every
  desk within **~15 m** of a window or interior biophilic zone.
- **Prospect (P11)** — open vistas across the floor, line of sight ≥ 6 m to
  support unconscious environmental scanning.
- **Refuge (P12)** — shielded, enclosed, back-against-surface seats for focus
  or recovery. Match every open-plan lounge with a set of refuge pockets.

### 1.2 Kellert framework — complementary view

Kellert, S. R., Heerwagen, J., & Mador, M. L. (Eds.) (2008). **Biophilic
Design : The Theory, Science and Practice of Bringing Buildings to Life**.
Wiley, Hoboken.

Kellert identified **70+ biophilic mechanisms**; in Kellert & Calabrese (2015)
these were consolidated into **24 attributes** across three experiences :
direct experience of nature, indirect experience of nature, and experience
of space and place.

## 2. Plants in the workspace

### 2.1 Nieuwenhuis, Knight, Postmes & Haslam 2014 — +15 % productivity

Nieuwenhuis, M., Knight, C., Postmes, T., & Haslam, S. A. (2014). **The
relative benefits of green versus lean office space : Three field
experiments**. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Applied*, 20(3), 199-214.

Three field experiments in UK and Dutch offices compared **lean** (sterile,
minimal) vs **green** (enriched with plants) workspaces on the same employees.

- Productivity improved by **~15 %** in the green condition versus the lean
  baseline.
- Workplace satisfaction, concentration, air-quality perception and feelings
  of involvement were also higher.
- Authors argue against the "minimalist" office doctrine and support
  physically enriching the environment.

**Common citation error** : this result is often attributed to Knight &
Haslam 2010. The 2010 paper ("The relative merits of lean, enriched, and
empowered offices", *JEP: Applied*, 16, 158-172) is the **earlier**
lab-based study; the 15 % figure comes from the **2014** field replication.

### 2.2 Planting density heuristic

Industry rule of thumb (Ambius, Interface, Planteria) : **1 plant per
2 – 3 m²** of open office area produces a visible biophilic effect.
`[À VÉRIFIER source primaire densité]`.

## 3. Restorative environments

### 3.1 Kaplan & Kaplan — Attention Restoration Theory (ART)

Kaplan, R., & Kaplan, S. (1989). **The Experience of Nature: A Psychological
Perspective**. Cambridge University Press.

ART posits that **directed attention** (cognitive control) is depleted by
sustained focus and can be **restored** by environments exhibiting four
properties :

- **Being-away** : sense of psychological escape
- **Fascination** : soft, involuntary attention (e.g. moving leaves)
- **Extent** : coherent depth, "another world"
- **Compatibility** : environment matches what the person wants to do

Office design implications : every building needs **restorative pockets**
(outdoor terrace, planted atrium, quiet loggia) that meet the four criteria.
The ratio of 1 restorative zone per 50 – 80 occupants is a workable planning
heuristic `[À VÉRIFIER]`.

### 3.2 Ulrich — Stress Recovery Theory (SRT)

Ulrich, R. S. (1983). **Aesthetic and affective response to natural
environment**. In Altman & Wohlwill (Eds.), *Human Behavior and Environment,
Vol. 6, Behavior and the Natural Environment*.

SRT predicts automatic, rapid physiological recovery from stress when
exposed to unthreatening natural scenes. Evidence base includes Ulrich's
1984 hospital-window study showing faster post-surgical recovery for
patients with a window view of trees (Ulrich, R. S., 1984, *Science*,
224(4647), 420-421).

## 4. Light, circadian rhythm and alertness

- **Horizontal illuminance at the eye** ≥ **250 melanopic EDI lux** during
  work hours supports circadian entrainment (WELL v2 Feature L03,
  `[À VÉRIFIER valeur à jour]`).
- Exposure to **morning daylight** (high CCT, > 5 500 K equivalent) before
  10 :00 correlates with better sleep onset at night (Figueiro et al.,
  several papers from Lighting Research Center).
- **Dynamic lighting** that drifts in CCT and intensity across the day is
  being adopted in premium fit-outs (Tunable white LED systems).

## 5. Density, noise, and cognitive performance

- **Open-plan office noise** reduces performance on complex cognitive tasks
  by an average of **~66 %** for memory tasks and ~20 % for reading
  comprehension in the classic meta-study `[À VÉRIFIER chiffres — Banbury
  & Berry 2005 et Hongisto]`.
- Leesman 2024 : noise is the worst-scoring dimension at **35 %** employee
  satisfaction.
- **Partial dividers and sound-absorbing ceilings** yield the largest per-€
  gains ; sound masking is a late-stage fix, not an early-stage substitute
  for absorption (see `design://acoustic-standards`).

## 6. Prospect–Refuge balance in open plan

From Appleton's habitat theory (1975) and later formalised by Browning et
al. :

- **Prospect** (open sight-lines) supports alertness and navigation
- **Refuge** (shielded nooks) supports focus, safety, recovery

Effective plans alternate them : an open collab cluster with a view
(prospect) abutting a lounge against a solid wall with a wing-back sofa
(refuge). Purely prospect-only plans (big open spaces) fatigue users; purely
refuge-only (boxy enclosed offices) feel oppressive.

## 7. Colour and materiality

Evidence on colour in offices is mixed. The robust finding is that
**low-chroma, high-luminance** palettes with **warm natural wood** support
calm and focus, while **highly saturated accents** should be used sparingly
for wayfinding and identity. `[À VÉRIFIER — évidence peer review]`
