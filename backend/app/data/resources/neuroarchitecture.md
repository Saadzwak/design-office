# Neuroarchitecture in the office

Neuroarchitecture is the bridge between neuroscience and the built
environment. For an office fit-out, it is the only credible way to defend
a design recommendation with something stronger than "it looks nice". This
document distils the design-relevant evidence into actionable cues.

**Leesman 2024 anchor** : across the Leesman global panel, offices score
**Lmi = 69.5**. Home-working scores **H-Lmi = 79.5**, a ten-point gap.
Closing the gap is a neuroarchitecture problem first, an aesthetics problem
second. The evidence below is what wins it back.

## 1. Biophilic design — foundational framework

### 1.1 Browning, Ryan & Clancy 2014 — 14 Patterns of Biophilic Design

Browning, W. D., Ryan, C. O., & Clancy, J. O. (2014). **14 Patterns of
Biophilic Design: Improving Health & Well-Being in the Built Environment**.
New York : Terrapin Bright Green LLC. A 10th-anniversary edition appeared in
2024.

The 14 patterns cluster into **three experiential categories**, with
documented physiological, cognitive and psychological effects :

#### Nature in the Space (patterns 1–7)

| # | Pattern                                    | Design move                                                        | Documented effect (Browning et al. summary) |
|---|--------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| 1 | Visual Connection with Nature              | Sight-line to living greenery or landscape from every regular seat | Reduced blood pressure, improved mood       |
| 2 | Non-Visual Connection with Nature          | Sounds (rain, wind, bird), scents, haptic natural materials        | Lower cortisol, improved cognitive performance |
| 3 | Non-Rhythmic Sensory Stimuli               | Leaves moving, cloud shadows, water ripples — stochastic stimuli   | Improved attention, positive affect          |
| 4 | Thermal & Airflow Variability              | Controllable operable windows, subtle air movement                 | Improved comfort, reduced fatigue            |
| 5 | Presence of Water                          | Fountain, still basin, mist wall                                   | Enhanced concentration, reduced stress       |
| 6 | Dynamic & Diffuse Light                    | Daylight variations, shadow play, tunable electric light           | Circadian regulation, alertness              |
| 7 | Connection with Natural Systems            | Exposed weather cues (rain on skylight, seasonal planting)         | Improved health perception, wellbeing        |

#### Natural Analogues (patterns 8–10)

| # | Pattern                           | Design move                                                      | Documented effect                           |
|---|-----------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| 8 | Biomorphic Forms & Patterns       | Organic curves, fractal patterns in carpet, ceiling baffles      | Perceptual engagement, stress reduction     |
| 9 | Material Connection with Nature   | Real wood, stone, cork, wool; avoid PVC wood-effect in primary zones | Aesthetic preference, longer dwell time |
| 10 | Complexity & Order                | Ordered-but-not-monotonous palette, fractal dimension D ≈ 1.3–1.5 (see §6) | Preference, stress reduction ↑ 60 % |

#### Nature of the Space (patterns 11–14)

| # | Pattern           | Design move                                                     | Documented effect                                    |
|---|-------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| 11 | Prospect         | Open sight-line ≥ 6 m, elevated viewpoint across the floor      | Reduced stress, boredom; improved comfort            |
| 12 | Refuge           | Shielded pockets, wing-back sofas, back-against-wall seats      | Reduced perceived risk, improved concentration        |
| 13 | Mystery          | Curved paths, partial reveals inviting exploration              | Pleasure response, increased dwell time              |
| 14 | Risk / Peril     | Controlled thrill — transparent floors, dramatic prospects       | Strong engagement (dopaminergic response)            |

**Planning implications for an office fit-out** :

- **Pattern 1 (Visual connection)** — target : every regularly occupied seat
  within ~7 – 8 m of a window or an interior biophilic anchor (planted
  wall, atrium view). 15 m absolute maximum ; deeper seats must borrow via
  glazed partitions.
- **Pattern 11 (Prospect)** — keep at least one sight-line of ≥ 15 m across
  the floor to avoid claustrophobic zones. In Lumen's 60 × 40 m plate,
  that's easy on the long axis, trickier across floors.
- **Pattern 12 (Refuge)** — every open collab cluster should be
  accompanied by a refuge pocket (high-back Alcove, library nook,
  telephone booth). Pure prospect-only zones fatigue users.

**Sources** :
- Terrapin Bright Green — [14 Patterns of Biophilic Design (2014 PDF)](https://www.terrapinbrightgreen.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/14-Patterns-of-Biophilic-Design-Terrapin-2014p.pdf)
- Terrapin Bright Green — [10th anniversary edition 2024](https://www.botanicus.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/14-Patterns-10th-anniv-ed-2024.pdf)
- Semantic Scholar — [bibliographic entry](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/14-Patterns-of-Biophilic-Design:-Improving-Health-Browning-Ryan/46451d655352680ebbae33965e43d93b1cacfee3)

### 1.2 Kellert framework — complementary view

Kellert, S. R., Heerwagen, J., & Mador, M. L. (Eds.) (2008). **Biophilic
Design: The Theory, Science and Practice of Bringing Buildings to Life**.
Hoboken, NJ : Wiley.

Kellert originally identified **more than 70 biophilic mechanisms**. With
Calabrese (2015), these consolidated into **24 attributes** organised
across three experiences :

- **Direct experience of nature** — light, air, water, plants, animals,
  weather, natural landscapes, fire
- **Indirect experience of nature** — images of nature, natural materials,
  natural colours, simulated natural light and air, biomimicry,
  biomorphy, geomorphology of place, evolved human–nature relationships
- **Experience of space and place** — prospect & refuge, organised
  complexity, integration of parts to wholes, transitional spaces,
  mobility & wayfinding, cultural & ecological attachment to place

Kellert's framework is more granular than Browning's and more useful for
**specification writing** — each attribute maps to a finishing palette,
material choice, or spatial move that an interior designer can tender.

**Sources** :
- Wiley — [Kellert, Heerwagen, Mador — Biophilic Design 2008](https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/Biophilic+Design:+The+Theory,+Science+and+Practice+of+Bringing+Buildings+to+Life-p-9780470163344)
- Kellert & Calabrese 2015 — [The Practice of Biophilic Design (PDF)](https://biophilicdesign.umn.edu/sites/biophilic-net-positive.umn.edu/files/2021-09/2015_Kellert%20_The_Practice_of_Biophilic_Design.pdf)

## 2. Plants in the workspace

### 2.1 Nieuwenhuis, Knight, Postmes & Haslam 2014 — +15 % productivity

Nieuwenhuis, M., Knight, C., Postmes, T., & Haslam, S. A. (2014). **The
relative benefits of green versus lean office space: Three field
experiments**. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Applied*, 20(3),
199–214.

- Three field experiments in UK and Dutch offices compared **lean**
  (sterile, minimalist) vs **green** (enriched with plants) workspaces
  on the same employees.
- Productivity rose **~15 %** in the green condition vs the lean baseline.
- Workplace satisfaction, concentration, air-quality perception, and
  feelings of involvement all increased.
- The authors explicitly argued against the "minimalist / lean" office
  doctrine and for physical enrichment of the workspace.

**Citation precision** : this result is often mis-attributed to Knight &
Haslam 2010. The 2010 paper ("The relative merits of lean, enriched, and
empowered offices", *JEP : Applied*, 16, 158–172) is the **earlier
laboratory** study ; the **15 %** figure comes from the **2014** field
replication. If the Justify surface is asked about this number, it MUST
cite 2014.

**Sources** :
- PubMed — [Nieuwenhuis et al. 2014 — Green vs lean office](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25068481/)
- APA PsycNet — [Nieuwenhuis et al. 2014 record](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2014-30837-001)
- University of Exeter — [Press coverage, Knight group 2014](https://news-archive.exeter.ac.uk/2014/september/title_409094_en.html)

### 2.2 Planting-density rule of thumb

Industry practice (Ambius, Interface, Planteria, Kinnarps) converges on
**one medium plant per 2 – 3 m²** of open office area for a **visible
biophilic effect** (i.e. every seat has a plant in the field of view).
Going below 1 plant per 5 m² is typically perceived as tokenistic
`[À VÉRIFIER source primaire de ce seuil]`.

A more robust rule : plants should occupy **3 – 5 % of floor area** in a
"moderate" biophilic regime, **5 – 8 %** in a "generous" one. See
`design://biophilic-office §1.1`.

### 2.3 Air-quality honest caveat

The "NASA Clean Air Study" (Wolverton et al. 1989) showing VOC absorption
by common houseplants is **not generalisable** to offices with normal
ventilation rates. Sealed chambers ≠ real buildings. Cummings & Waring
(2020, *Journal of Exposure Science & Environmental Epidemiology*)
analysed 196 air-quality experiments and showed the effective VOC
removal rate from typical office plant densities is negligible compared
to the building's own air exchange rate.

Plants deliver **psychological, biophilic, acoustic (slightly)** benefits.
Do not sell them as an air-quality intervention to an informed client —
it is a credibility trap.

**Sources** :
- Cummings & Waring 2020 — [Potted plants do not improve indoor air quality](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41370-019-0175-9) `[À VÉRIFIER DOI]`
- Wolverton et al. 1989 — NASA Clean Air Study (historical context only)

## 3. Attention restoration and stress recovery

### 3.1 Kaplan & Kaplan — Attention Restoration Theory (ART)

Kaplan, R., & Kaplan, S. (1989). **The Experience of Nature: A
Psychological Perspective**. Cambridge : Cambridge University Press.

ART posits that **directed attention** — the effortful cognitive control
needed to focus on a specific task and ignore distraction — is depleted
by sustained mental work, and can be **restored** by environments that
exhibit **four properties** :

1. **Being-away** — sense of psychological escape, physically or
   metaphorically, from habitual demands
2. **Fascination (soft)** — stimuli that capture attention effortlessly,
   leaving directed attention free to recover (leaves moving in wind,
   fireplace, water)
3. **Extent** — coherence and depth, the sense of being in "another
   world" large enough to explore cognitively
4. **Compatibility** — the environment matches what the person wants to
   do there

**Soft fascination** is typically considered the key driver; the other
three properties enhance or sustain it.

#### Empirical support and caveats

A 2016 systematic review (Stevenson, Schilhab & Bentsen, *Environmental
Research and Public Health*) included 31 studies. Meta-analyses found
significant positive effects of natural-environment exposure on three
attention measures (Digit Span Forward, Digit Span Backward, Trail
Making Test B). Evidence across other attention tests was mixed.

**Honest summary** : ART is **theoretically sound**, **well-cited** (≈
10 000+ citations on Google Scholar), and the most actionable lens for
designing office "restorative pockets". The empirical base is
**inconclusive** in strength outside the three consistent measures
above — write argumentaires that lean on the framework's design
implications, not on over-stated productivity claims.

**Planning heuristic** : **one restorative zone per 50 – 80 occupants**
(outdoor terrace, planted atrium, quiet loggia, wellness room). Zone
must meet all four ART criteria, or it is decorative, not restorative.

**Sources** :
- Kaplan & Kaplan 1989 — *The Experience of Nature*, Cambridge UP `[book, no canonical URL]`
- Wikipedia — [Attention Restoration Theory (entry)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attention_restoration_theory)
- Tandfonline — [Stevenson et al. 2018 systematic review](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10937404.2018.1505571)

### 3.2 Ulrich 1984 — Stress Recovery Theory, window-view study

Ulrich, R. S. (1984). **View through a window may influence recovery
from surgery**. *Science*, 224(4647), 420–421.

- Retrospective analysis of cholecystectomy patients in a Pennsylvania
  suburban hospital, 1972–1981.
- **23 patients** assigned to rooms looking out on a natural scene were
  matched with 23 patients in rooms facing a brick wall.
- Natural-view patients had **shorter postoperative stays**, **fewer
  negative nursing notes**, and required **fewer potent analgesics**.

This is the most-cited single evidence-based design paper ; alongside it,
Ulrich's **Stress Recovery Theory (SRT, 1983)** predicts automatic,
rapid physiological recovery from stress on exposure to non-threatening
natural scenes (mechanism : parasympathetic engagement, cortisol and
blood-pressure reduction within minutes).

For an office, SRT implies that **brief, repeated nature exposures**
(every 60–90 min break at a planted zone, a view through a window on
the commute to a meeting) matter more than one-off gesture installations.

**Sources** :
- PubMed — [Ulrich 1984 abstract](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6143402/)
- Science — [View Through a Window, DOI 10.1126/science.6143402](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.6143402)
- Ulrich 1983 — *Aesthetic and affective response to natural environment*
  in Altman & Wohlwill, *Human Behavior and Environment, Vol. 6*

### 3.3 Cortisol reduction from nature exposure

Several lab and field studies report **cortisol reduction** of 10 – 15 %
after 20 – 30 min in a wooded environment vs an urban control (Park,
Tsunetsugu et al., 2010, "The physiological effects of Shinrin-yoku /
forest bathing", *Environmental Health and Preventive Medicine*).
In-office biophilic exposure doesn't match a real forest, but evidence
supports direction of effect : **incorporating nature elements dampens
physiological stress responses**.

**Sources** :
- Park et al. 2010 — [Shinrin-yoku cortisol evidence](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19568835/) `[À VÉRIFIER DOI exact]`

### 3.4 Heerwagen & Orians — Savanna Hypothesis

Heerwagen and Orians proposed (1986, 1993) that humans are
**evolutionarily predisposed** to prefer certain types of natural
landscapes resembling the African savanna (open fields with scattered
trees, water in view, varied ground cover). The hypothesis is part of
the theoretical underpinning of biophilic design and the prospect–refuge
pairing.

**Practical implication** : biophilic office zones should echo savanna
archetypes — a central clearing (collab), scattered canopies (acoustic
"trees"), refuge edges (lounge), and sight-lines to water or greenery.
This isn't decoration ; it's tuning the design to an evolved perceptual
preference.

**Sources** : Heerwagen, J. H., & Orians, G. H. (1993). *Humans,
habitats, and aesthetics*. In Kellert & Wilson (Eds.), *The Biophilia
Hypothesis*. Island Press. `[book, no URL]`

## 4. Light, circadian rhythm and alertness

### 4.1 Circadian lighting targets

- **≥ 250 melanopic EDI lux at the eye** during work hours supports
  circadian entrainment (WELL Building Standard v2 Feature L03 — see
  IWBI documentation).
- **Morning bright light** (high CCT, > 5 500 K equivalent, high
  melanopic content) before 10 am correlates with earlier sleep onset
  and better sleep quality at night.
- **Tunable-white electric lighting** that drifts in CCT and intensity
  across the day is the engineering answer to fixed north-facing
  floors or deep-plan layouts where daylight cannot reach the back.

**Sources** :
- IWBI — WELL v2 Light concept `[À VÉRIFIER latest v2 pilot]`
- Lighting Research Center — Figueiro et al., multiple papers on
  circadian lighting

### 4.2 Window proximity and wellbeing

Leesman 2024 : **47 %** of employees satisfied with "plants and
greenery" in the office ; **41 %** with temperature control ; these two
correlate tightly with perceived connection to the outside.

Heerwagen summary data (cited in *Biophilic Design*, 2008) : employees
with a window view report **better job satisfaction** and **fewer
reports of headache / fatigue** than employees seated > 6 m from a
window, controlling for job type.

Planning rule : **no regular seat more than 15 m from a window**,
preferably **≤ 8 m**. For Lumen's 60 × 40 m plate, that means no seat
deeper than 20 m into the plate (half-depth) — well within reach if
workstations hug the façades and services/collab are central.

## 5. Density, noise and cognitive performance

See `design://acoustic-standards` for the full acoustic framework. The
neuroarchitecture view :

### 5.1 Hongisto — speech intelligibility is the driver

Hongisto, V. (2005). **A model predicting the effect of speech of
varying intelligibility on work performance**. *Indoor Air*, 15(6),
458–468.

Key finding : in open-plan offices, **it is not the absolute sound
level of speech that degrades cognitive performance, but its
intelligibility** — operationalised as the Speech Transmission Index
(STI).

- Performance starts to decrease when **STI > 0.20**.
- Peak performance decrement is reached at **STI ≈ 0.60** in the
  original 2005 model.
- A 2020 revised model (Haapakangas et al., *Indoor Air*) found a
  tighter range : performance drops above **STI ≈ 0.21** and plateaus
  at **STI ≈ 0.44**.
- Verbal short-term memory tasks are most strongly affected.

**Performance loss magnitude** : the literature reports **4 – 45 %**
performance degradation depending on task type, with verbal memory
tasks worst-hit.

### 5.2 Industry outcome

Aisti / AkustikLab summary (Hongisto-informed) : offices with poor
acoustics are reported to be **~16 % less productive** than offices
with good acoustics. This is not a single peer-reviewed number — it's
an industry rollup of Hongisto-class studies — but it is defensible to
cite with the caveat "industry aggregate from Hongisto-model papers".

**Design implications** :
- Reduce STI **at the receiver's position** by combining ceiling
  absorption, partial partitions (1.4 – 1.6 m), and sound masking (pink
  noise at ~45 dBA).
- Separate **focus zones from collab zones** by at least one full
  partition or by distance (~10 m) to bring STI < 0.20.

**Sources** :
- PubMed — [Hongisto 2005 model](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16268835/)
- ICBEN 2008 — [Hongisto task performance and speech intelligibility (PDF)](https://www.icben.org/2008/PDFs/Hongisto_et_al.pdf)
- Indoor Air 2020 — [Haapakangas et al. revised model](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/ina.12726)

## 6. Prospect–Refuge balance

From Appleton's **habitat theory** (1975, *The Experience of Landscape*,
Wiley) and formalised by Browning et al. (P11/P12) :

- **Prospect** — open sight-lines, elevated viewpoint — supports
  alertness, spatial awareness, and exploratory behaviour
- **Refuge** — shielded, enclosed, back-against-surface seats — supports
  focus, recovery, safety perception

**Effective open-plan plans alternate them**. An open collab cluster
with a façade view (prospect) abutting a lounge with wing-back sofas
against a solid wall (refuge). Pure prospect-only plans (big open
spaces) fatigue users ; pure refuge-only plans (boxy enclosed
everything) feel oppressive and generate "cave" isolation.

In planning terms : every open collab island should sit within ~10 m
of a refuge pocket, and every quartier of workstations should have
both at least one prospect axis (long sight-line) and one refuge
pocket (booth, high-back sofa, library-like corner).

## 7. Colour, materiality, pattern complexity

### 7.1 Fractal dimension D ≈ 1.3 – 1.5

Taylor, R. P., and colleagues at the University of Oregon have shown
that patterns with **mid-range fractal dimension (D = 1.3 – 1.5)**
trigger the strongest physiological stress-reduction response. The
effect is measurable in heart-rate variability and skin conductance,
and holds whether the pattern is a real tree canopy, a Jackson Pollock
painting (which reliably falls in this range), or a designed fractal
carpet.

Reported stress-reduction magnitudes up to **~60 %** in lab settings.

**Design implications** :
- Acoustic ceiling baffles and wall panels with **fractal perforation
  patterns** (several commercial ranges meet the D ≈ 1.4 target)
- **Carpet tiles** with organic, fractal shapes (Interface's Biophilic
  series was designed on this basis)
- **Wayfinding signage** using organic, non-grid curves

**Sources** :
- Taylor, R. P. — [Fractals in psychology and art (UO blog)](https://blogs.uoregon.edu/richardtaylor/2016/02/03/human-physiological-responses-to-fractals-in-nature-and-art/)
- Taylor & Juliani — [Fractal Fluency for biophilic architecture](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-Implications-of-Fractal-Fluency-for-Biophilic-Taylor-Juliani/a91aebfbc1230eb1935e2f615f8a0651ba6e2c69)

### 7.2 Colour evidence — honest summary

The office-colour literature is **mixed and often poorly designed**
(small samples, self-report dependent variables, no pre-registration).
The robust findings :

- **Low-chroma, high-luminance** palettes with **warm natural wood**
  support calm, focus, perceived comfort
- **Highly saturated accents** should be used sparingly — for
  wayfinding, identity, and energising zones (café, event)
- **Green and blue-green** are most reliably associated with calm and
  cognitive ease ; **red** is associated with energy, also with
  increased error rates on detail-oriented tasks (but evidence is
  ambivalent)

`[À VÉRIFIER — recommand peer-reviewed source for red-error claim]`.

Treat colour as identity + biophilic integrity before as productivity
lever.

## 8. Biophilic certifications and frameworks

- **WELL Building Standard v2** (IWBI) — the Mind concept includes
  Features dedicated to biophilic integration. Most comprehensive
  wellbeing certification.
- **Fitwel** (CDC + GSA) — scorecard for building and tenant fit-outs,
  lighter-touch than WELL, includes outdoor views and indoor plants
- **Living Building Challenge** (ILFI) — "Biophilic Environment"
  imperative under Petal 07 Health & Happiness
- **BREEAM / LEED** — neither has a biophilic-design dedicated credit,
  but daylight + views credits correlate

For a French Cat B fit-out, **WELL v2 Silver** is the typical stretch
target ; Lumen's brief language ("zones calmes", "cafétéria pas
reléguée", "expression marque forte") maps cleanly onto WELL Mind + L03
Circadian Lighting + S (Sound) features.

## 10. What to cite in an argumentaire — shortlist

When writing for the client (Justify surface), prefer :

1. **Browning 14 Patterns (2014, 10th anniv 2024)** — for any biophilic claim
2. **Nieuwenhuis et al. 2014** — for the +15 % productivity plants figure
   (NEVER cite 2010 for this number)
3. **Ulrich 1984** — for the view-to-nature recovery effect
4. **Hongisto 2005 / Haapakangas 2020** — for the acoustic-productivity link
5. **Leesman 2024** — for aggregated employee-experience benchmarks
6. **Kellert, Heerwagen, Mador 2008** — for the foundational framework

Use `[À VÉRIFIER]` in the argumentaire itself whenever the source is a
secondary summary (industry report, trade press) rather than the
underlying peer-reviewed paper. The client conversations that go
sideways are always the ones where an unsourced "+15 %" is challenged.
