# Biophilic office — implementation guide

## 1. Planting

### 1.1 Density

Working planning rules, consistent with Ambius / Interface / Planteria
practice guides :

| Intervention level | Plant footprint as % of floor area | Notes                                      |
|--------------------|------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| Discrete           | 1 – 2 %                            | Reception and café accent only             |
| Moderate           | 3 – 5 %                            | Distributed across all zones, visible from every desk cluster |
| Generous           | 5 – 8 %                            | Planted walls + floor planters, maintenance in contract |
| Immersive          | 8 % +                              | Atrium gardens, mature trees, biophilic anchor           |

`[À VÉRIFIER source primaire pour ces seuils — heuristique industrie,
pas norme]`

Another common rule : **1 medium plant per 2 – 3 m²** of open office floor.

### 1.2 Species by light level

Offices vary dramatically in natural-light delivery; pick species to match
the zone :

| Light environment                         | Species examples                                        |
|-------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| Direct sun (south window, no shade)       | *Ficus elastica*, *Strelitzia*, *Yucca*                |
| Bright indirect (within 3 m of window)    | *Ficus lyrata*, *Monstera deliciosa*, *Dracaena*       |
| Medium indirect (3 – 6 m from window)     | *Epipremnum aureum* (Pothos), *Philodendron*, *Aglaonema* |
| Low / artificial only                     | *Zamioculcas*, *Sansevieria*, *Chlorophytum*           |
| Green walls (hydroponic, artificial LED)  | Mixed ferns, Peperomia, Fittonia                       |

### 1.3 Maintenance and cost

- **Cost of entry-level planting** : 8 – 20 €/m² of open floor, installed
  `[À VÉRIFIER bench]`
- **Green wall** : 800 – 1 800 €/m² of wall, installed + irrigation
  `[À VÉRIFIER]`
- **Maintenance contract** : typically 10 – 20 % of install cost per year
- **Water supply and drainage** must be planned for walls with > 10 m² of
  planted area

### 1.4 Air-quality disclaimer

The "NASA Clean Air Study" often cited to justify plants for VOC removal
was a 1989 study in sealed chambers — results do **not** generalise to
office ventilation rates. Plants deliver psychological and biophilic
benefits; do not sell them as an air-quality solution.
Reference : Cummings & Waring (2020), *Potted plants do not improve indoor
air quality : a review and analysis of reported VOC removal efficiencies*,
*Journal of Exposure Science & Environmental Epidemiology*.
`[À VÉRIFIER citation exacte DOI]`

## 2. Natural light access

- Prioritise orientation of workstations so that **every seat has a
  sight-line to a window within ~7 – 8 m** (15 m maximum)
- **Glazed partitions** between open floor and enclosed rooms to borrow light
- **Skylights / atria** for deep floors; a 300 m² atrium can condition a
  full deep-plan floor's psychological perception of "openness"
- **Window treatment** : mechanical blinds with user control at the
  workstation, not only central control

Reference circadian target (from WELL v2) : **≥ 250 melanopic EDI lux at
the eye during work hours**. In winter, north façades typically deliver
< 100 melanopic EDI at 3 m from the window — the gap is filled by tunable
white LED systems on a circadian schedule.

## 3. Natural materials

Favour :

- **Solid or engineered wood** : oak, ash, walnut, birch. Visible grain.
- **Stone accents** : marble, travertine, slate — sparingly, at high-impact
  points
- **Raw textiles** : wool, linen, leather
- **Clay / concrete brut**
- **Cork** on walls or ceilings (acoustic + tactile)

Avoid :

- PVC wood-effect laminate in client-facing areas (the tactile break is
  perceived as "fake")
- Uniform high-gloss surfaces that flatten the material vocabulary
- Pure white or cold grey-only palettes

## 4. Water

Water features (fountain, mist wall, still basin) are effective biophilic
anchors (Browning P5 : Presence of Water). Practical limits in office :

- Plumbing and evaporative-load planning
- Legionella risk mitigation for standing water
- Maintenance : weekly cleaning minimum

Budget a small still-water feature (50 – 100 L) in the café or atrium, not
scattered installations.

## 5. Fractal patterns, biomorphic forms

Browning P8 "Biomorphic forms & patterns" : patterns with a fractal
dimension **D ≈ 1.3 – 1.5** have been shown in Taylor et al. research to
reduce physiological stress (fractals analogous to those in nature such as
tree canopies, coastlines, clouds). Application :

- Acoustic panels with fractal perforation patterns
- Carpet tiles with organic shapes (e.g. Interface Biophilic series)
- Wayfinding signage using organic, non-grid curves

## 6. Complexity and order (Browning P10)

Environments that are **ordered but not monotonous** score best. Typical
planning move :

- Neighbourhoods coherent in palette and materiality, but distinct across
  the floor (one warm oak + green zone, one mineral + grey-blue zone)
- Random-organic arrangement of planters, not grid lines
- Art and texture breaks every ~10 – 15 m of open floor

## 7. Thermal and airflow variability (Browning P4)

Strictly uniform temperature and no airflow is boring and fatiguing. Subtle
variability (windows that open, small ceiling fans, occupant-controlled
vents) delivers measurable benefit.

- Provide **occupant controls** within ~3 m of every work area
- Locate workstations so that at least one zone has access to operable
  windows if the façade permits

## 8. Sensory stimuli — "non-rhythmic" (Browning P3)

Examples :

- Leaves moving in the wind (window with planting outside)
- Birdsong audible through an open door to the terrace
- Fountain sound varying in amplitude

These stimulate fascination (ART) without engaging directed attention.
Scheduled piped sound is **not** a substitute — predictable patterns do not
trigger the same restoration response.

## 9. Prospect & refuge as spatial doctrine

(See `design://neuroarchitecture` §6.) Every biophilic zone should alternate
prospect (open sight-line, elevated viewpoint) and refuge (enclosed
pockets, high-back sofas, canopy elements). Pure prospect zones feel
exposed; pure refuge zones feel claustrophobic.

## 10. Measurement and KPIs

Post-occupancy, track :

- % of seats with a direct window sight-line
- Plant count density (plants / 100 m²)
- Employee perceived "connection with nature" (Leesman or custom survey)
- Biophilic pattern count — a floor should express at least 8 of 14
  Browning patterns to meet "Very Good" biophilic quality `[À VÉRIFIER —
  target 8/14 est une heuristique, pas une norme]`
